Your Personalized Sleep Report

Overview

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) occurs when the airway repeatedly narrows or collapses during sleep, causing breathing pauses and drops in oxygen levels. These interruptions can fragment sleep, lead to daytime tiredness, and over time increase risks for high blood pressure, heart disease, and stroke. This report explains your results and treatment options in plain language.

Your Sleep Test Results

Here’s what your sleep test shows and what each number means. Normal ranges are included for comparison.

AHI (Apnea–Hypopnea Index)Average number of breathing pauses per hour. Normal: less than 5. Higher values mean more frequent interruptions.
ODI (Oxygen Desaturation Index)How many times per hour your oxygen drops by at least 4%. Normal: less than 5. Higher values indicate more oxygen stress.
Oxygen NadirThe lowest oxygen level during sleep. Normal: above 90%. Lower numbers suggest significant desaturations.
Hypoxic BurdenMeasures total time spent with low oxygen. Normal: under 60%·min/h. Higher levels can strain the heart over time.
BMI / Neck SizeBody and neck size can affect airway crowding. A neck larger than 17 inches (men) increases OSA risk.

Your Main Findings

  • High Anatomical Contribution: Your airway is narrower than average (due to weight, tonsils, or jaw structure), which makes it easier to collapse during sleep.
  • Low Arousal Threshold: You wake easily when breathing changes, leading to disrupted, unrefreshing sleep.
  • High Hypoxic Burden: Your oxygen levels drop frequently at night, which can strain the heart and blood vessels.
  • Positional OSA: Your apnea is worse when you sleep on your back. Side-sleeping can help reduce events.

What This Means for You

Your test shows evidence of sleep apnea. Treating it can improve sleep quality, focus, and long-term cardiovascular health.

  1. CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure): A bedside machine that gently blows air through a mask to keep your airway open all night. It is the most effective and commonly recommended treatment for OSA.
  2. Custom Oral Appliance: A dentist-fitted mouthpiece that moves your lower jaw slightly forward, helping to keep your airway open—often effective for mild to moderate OSA or CPAP intolerance.
  3. Positional Therapy: Techniques or devices that train you to sleep on your side to reduce breathing events when lying on your back.
  4. Nasal Treatments: Addressing nasal congestion with saline, steroid sprays, or, if needed, simple procedures like septoplasty or turbinate reduction can improve airflow and comfort with CPAP.
  5. Surgery: Certain patients benefit from procedures to enlarge or stabilize the airway, such as tonsillectomy, palate surgery, or Inspire (hypoglossal nerve stimulation) therapy.
